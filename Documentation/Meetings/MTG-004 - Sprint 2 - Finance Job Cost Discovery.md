Meeting Objective

The objective of this workshop was to understand the financial management, job cost, work-in-progress (WIP), forecasting, and project review processes used within a commercial construction company. The discussion focused on how financial information supports operational decision-making and how these processes should be represented within the Construction Analytics Solution.

Discussion Summary
1. Role of Job Cost

Job Cost is responsible for ensuring that project costs are accurately assigned to the correct project, phase, cost code, and cost type. While Accounting focuses on recording vendor invoices, payroll, and financial transactions accurately, Job Cost ensures that those transactions are posted to the correct operational categories and compared against project budgets.

Job Cost also validates purchase orders, reviews budget versus actual performance, and investigates unusual variances before financial information is finalized.

2. Daily Review Process

The daily review process focused primarily on validating operational information before payroll and accounting processes were completed.

Typical daily activities included:

    Reviewing jobs worked the previous day.
    Reviewing preliminary payroll entries.
    Reviewing equipment entries for unusual hours or usage.
    Reviewing material quantities for potential posting errors.
    Identifying jobs missing labor, equipment, or material entries.
    Verifying that costs were being charged to active jobs.

Rather than reviewing dashboards, daily work consisted primarily of validating information across multiple operational systems.

3. Weekly Responsibilities

Weekly responsibilities included:

    Final payroll review.
    Equipment cost posting.
    Material posting review.
    Reopening jobs when additional work occurred.
    Budget adjustments when necessary.
    Preparation of weekly WIP reports.
    Closed job reporting.
    Weekly division performance reporting.
    Monthly forecasting.
    Sales commission reporting.
    Foreman incentive reporting.
    Division incentive reporting.
    Assistance with month-end accounting close.

4. Job Performance Review

Project investigations followed a consistent analytical process.

    Review overall budget versus actual performance.
    Review project phase summaries.
    Review cost code summaries.
    Review cost type performance.
    Investigate posting errors.
    Investigate operational causes.
    Communicate findings to Project Managers, Superintendents, Sales Representatives, or Estimators.

Labor was always reviewed first because labor performance frequently influenced burden and equipment costs.

5. Work In Progress (WIP)

The WIP workbook maintained information including:

    Contract Value
    Approved Change Orders
    Original Budget
    Current Budget
    Prior Month Revenue
    Current Period Costs
    Revenue Earned
    Percent Complete
    Remaining Budget
    Over/Under Billing
    Forecast Margin

Revenue earned was determined using the percentage of costs incurred relative to the estimated total project cost.

6. Budget Management

Budgets were adjusted only when justified by:

    Approved Change Orders
    Estimating Errors
    Field Conditions
    Superintendent-directed sequencing changes

Budget increases were made as soon as the additional costs became known to avoid significant project close-out adjustments.

Remaining budget values were generally not reduced until project completion.

7. Executive Reporting

Weekly executive reporting included:

    Company WIP Summary
    Division Performance
    Weekly Revenue
    Month-to-Date Revenue
    Gross Margin
    Forecast Revenue
    Forecast Gross Margin
    Labor Burden Recovery ("Flip")
    Equipment Cost Recovery ("Equipment Flip")
    Warranty Cost Reporting

Forecasts were generated using average month-to-date performance projected across the remaining weeks of the month.

8. Closed Job Reviews

Each week approximately 8–10 completed projects were selected for detailed review.

The review process emphasized learning rather than simply identifying errors.

Projects were evaluated using the following order:

    Fade/Gain Status
    Magnitude of Variance
    Labor Performance
    Material Quantity Variance
    Material Cost Variance
    Warranty Costs
    Cost Code Performance

Projects exceeding approximately 10% variance received detailed investigation.

9. Reporting Workflow

Weekly executive scorecards summarized company and division performance.

Supporting spreadsheets allowed users to progressively drill into:

    Division
    Project
    Phase
    Cost Code
    Transaction Detail

Filtered reports were distributed to:

    Division Managers
    Project Managers
    Sales Representatives
    Superintendents

Each stakeholder received information relevant to their area of responsibility.

Key Outcomes
    Job Cost serves as both a financial analysis and financial quality assurance function.
    Forecasting is more valuable than historical reporting for operational decision-making.
    Closed job reviews provide continuous improvement opportunities.
    Labor performance is the strongest leading indicator of project profitability.
    Executive reporting should emphasize trends, forecasts, and operational performance rather than detailed accounting transactions.
