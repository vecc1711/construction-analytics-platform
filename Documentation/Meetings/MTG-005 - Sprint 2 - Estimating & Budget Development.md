MTG-005 – Estimating & Budget Development

Meeting ID: MTG-005
Project: Construction Analytics Solution
Date: July 2026
Status: Complete

Meeting Objective

    The purpose of this workshop was to understand how project estimates are developed, how estimates become operational budgets, how production standards are established, and how estimating supports project execution throughout the project lifecycle.

Discussion Summary
1. Business Development
    The majority of projects originate from the Sales Team.

Approximately:

    -   4,000–6,000 quotes are prepared annually.
    -   Sales close rate averages 50–60%.
    -   Approximately 80–85% of awarded work comes from repeat customers.

    Projects are typically awarded based on customer relationships, service, and reputation rather than lowest price.

2. Estimate Preparation
Estimators review:

    - Sales scope
    - Site diagrams
    - Field notes
    - Site photographs
    - Customer discussions

    Construction drawings are used when available but most projects are owner-direct work.

3. Estimating Process
The estimating process follows a structured workflow.

    1.  Review sales scope.
    2.  Review site information.
    3.  Validate project scope with Sales.
    4.  Build project budget.
    5.  Add missing work items.
    6.  Select crew.
    7. Select equipment.
    8. Select supplier.
    9. Obtain subcontractor quotations when required.
    10. Determine production rates.
    11. Calculate labor hours.
    12. Calculate material quantities.
    13. Establish project margin.
    14. Finalize proposal.
    15. Return proposal to Sales.

4. Cost Codes
Cost Codes are standardized across the organization.
The same Cost Codes are used throughout:
    -     Estimating
    -     Job Budget
    -     Production
    -     Job Cost
    -     Reporting
Minor adjustments may be made when incidental work cannot be practically separated.

5. Production Standards
Production rates are developed using:
    - Historical performance
    - Estimator experience
    - Superintendent experience
    - Crew capability
    - Site conditions
    - Project complexity

Each project is evaluated individually.
Smaller projects may utilize standardized estimating tools with embedded production assumptions.

6. Labor Planning
Labor hours are determined using:
    - Standard crew configurations
    - Production rates
    - Crew size
    - Mobilization requirements
    - Travel time

Travel planning is based upon mobilization frequency and production hours.

7. Equipment Planning

Most crews have standard equipment packages.
Additional equipment is added when required based upon project conditions.

8. Material Estimating
Major materials are estimated using:
    - Project-specific quantities
    - Current supplier pricing

Examples include:
    - Asphalt
    - Concrete
    - Sealer
    - Crack filler
    - Pavement marking paint

Minor materials utilize historical usage assumptions.

9. Risk Considerations
Primary estimating risks include:

    - Existing pavement thickness
    - Weather
    - Traffic
    - Unknown site conditions

Significant unforeseen conditions are addressed through Change Orders whenever possible.

10. Project Handoff
Following project award, the following information is distributed:
    - Proposal
    - Job Budget
    - Site Diagram
    - Job Notes

Recipients include:
    - Project Manager
    - Superintendent
    - Foreman
    - Project Coordinator

11. Budget Development

The original estimate is reviewed prior to becoming the official project budget.

During this review:
    - Revenue is verified.
    - Scope changes are incorporated.
    - Gross Margin percentages are normalized across line items.

The reviewed budget becomes the baseline used throughout project execution.

12. Feedback Loop
Closed Job Reviews provide continuous feedback to estimating.
Lessons learned improve:
    - Production assumptions
    - Labor expectations
    - Material quantities
    - Future estimates

Key Outcomes
    Estimating establishes the operational execution plan.
    Production standards are based on both historical data and professional judgment.
    Standard crews provide consistency across estimating and operations.
    Estimates evolve into controlled project budgets.
    Continuous improvement is achieved through structured closed-job reviews.
